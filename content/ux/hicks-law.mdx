## Overview

Hick's Law describes the relationship between the number of choices available to a person and the time it takes them to make a decision. More choices means more time — not linearly, but logarithmically. Each doubling of options adds a roughly constant increment of decision time.

The principle was established by British psychologist W.E. Hick in 1952 and later refined by Ray Hyman, which is why it is sometimes called the Hick-Hyman Law.

## Why It Matters

Designers often equate more features with more value. Hick's Law challenges this directly. Every option added to a menu, form, or screen increases the cognitive cost of using it. Simplification is not the removal of capability — it is the reduction of decision burden.
