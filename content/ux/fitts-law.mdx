## Overview

Fitts' Law is a predictive model of human movement that describes the relationship between the size of a target, the distance to that target, and the time required to reach it. Originally formulated by psychologist Paul Fitts in 1954, it remains one of the most reliably validated principles in human-computer interaction.

The core insight is simple: targets that are larger and closer are faster to acquire. Targets that are small or far away demand more time and produce more errors.

## Why It Matters

In interface design, every interactive element is a target — buttons, links, form fields, checkboxes, sliders. Fitts' Law gives designers a principled basis for sizing and positioning these elements rather than relying on aesthetics alone.

<Callout type='insight' title='Key Takeaway'>
  The two variables that matter most are target size and distance.
  Optimize both, not just one.
</Callout>

Small, densely packed controls are not just an aesthetic problem. They measurably slow users down and increase error rates.
